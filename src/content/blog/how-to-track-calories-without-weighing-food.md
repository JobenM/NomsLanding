## You Don't Need a Scale to Track Calories

Food scales are precise. They're also inconvenient, easy to forget, impossible to use at restaurants, and — for most people — a fast track to giving up on tracking entirely.

The goal of calorie tracking isn't perfection. It's awareness. And there are several reliable methods for building that awareness without weighing a single gram.

## 1. Visual Portion References

Your hand is a surprisingly consistent portion guide, because it scales with your body size:

- **Palm** = roughly 85–100g of protein (chicken, fish, meat)
- **Closed fist** = roughly 200–250ml, useful for grains, pasta, or liquid
- **Cupped hand** = a portion of nuts, seeds, or dried fruit
- **Thumb tip** = roughly a teaspoon of oil, butter, or nut butter

These aren't exact — but they're consistent. Use them the same way each time and your tracking becomes meaningful even if it's not laboratory-precise.

## 2. The Plate Method

Divide your plate visually:
- Half vegetables
- A quarter protein
- A quarter carbs

This doesn't give you a calorie number, but it puts you in the right range for a balanced meal without any measuring. If you know your rough calorie target, you can use this alongside a simple estimate for your protein and carb portions.

## 3. Restaurant and Packaged Food as Anchors

Packaged foods have labels. Restaurant meals often have published nutrition info. These give you real reference points you can apply to home cooking.

If you know a standard portion of cooked rice from a restaurant is around 200 kcal, that anchors your estimate when you serve rice at home. You don't need to weigh it — you're building a mental model of what portions look like.

## 4. Describe It, Don't Measure It

This is where AI changes the equation. Instead of searching a database and hoping the entry matches what you actually ate, you describe your meal in plain language:

*"Bowl of porridge with a sliced banana and a tablespoon of honey"*

## 5. Log Everything Roughly Rather Than Some Things Precisely

The biggest mistake people make is tracking only when they can be exact. They log their prepped meals carefully and then skip logging the handful of crisps, the coffee with oat milk, or the post-work biscuit.

Those omissions add up faster than slightly imprecise estimates. Research consistently shows that inconsistent tracking produces worse outcomes than consistent-but-approximate tracking.

An imperfect log of everything beats a perfect log of half your day.

## How Accurate Is Good Enough?

For most people looking to manage their weight or improve their diet, being within 100–150 kcal of your actual intake is sufficient to see the trends that matter: whether you're eating roughly at maintenance, in a deficit, or in a surplus.

Weighing food can get you to ±20 kcal. Mindful estimation with consistent methods gets you to ±100–150 kcal. The difference in outcome over a week is negligible. The difference in compliance over a month is enormous.
